1.1 Global food insecurity and the need for alternative methods of food production.

Global food insecurity is a pressing issue that has been exacerbated by various factors, including population growth, climate change, and limited natural resources. In recent years, the need for alternative methods of food production has become increasingly apparent in order to mitigate these challenges.

According to the Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO), approximately 690 million people worldwide suffered from hunger in 2019, representing an increase of 60 million compared to the previous year. Additionally, the COVID-19 pandemic has further worsened the situation, with an estimated additional 132 million people experiencing acute food insecurity in 2020.[1]

The conventional methods of food production, which rely heavily on the use of chemicals, fertilizers, and pesticides, have significant negative impacts on the environment, such as soil degradation, water pollution, and greenhouse gas emissions. Adopting alternative methods of food production, therefore, is critical to ensure sustainable food systems while reducing environmental damage.[2]

One promising approach is vertical farming, which involves growing crops in vertically stacked layers using artificial lighting and controlled environments. This method can increase crop yields by up to 10 times compared to traditional farming methods, while reducing water usage by up to 90%. Moreover, it can be implemented in urban areas, reducing the distance between the point of production and consumption, thus minimizing the carbon footprint associated with transportation.[3]

Another alternative method is aquaponics, which combines aquaculture and hydroponics, allowing for the cultivation of both fish and plants in a closed-loop system. This technique uses only 10% of the water required for traditional farming methods while producing more food per unit area. Furthermore, the waste produced by the fish serves as nutrients for the plants, reducing the need for chemical fertilizers.[4]

In addition to these innovative approaches, there is also a growing interest in biofortification, which involves enhancing the nutritional value of crops through breeding or genetic modification. This method could help address malnutrition and related health issues, particularly in developing regions where access to nutritious food is limited.[5]

In conclusion, global food insecurity remains a major challenge, and alternative methods of food production are necessary to address the issue. Vertical farming, aquaponics, and biofortification are just a few examples of innovative techniques that can help ensure sustainable, efficient, and nutritious food systems while mitigating environmental damage.

1.2 The popularity of microgreens due to their nutritional benefits, ease of cultivation, and suitability for indoor growing.

Microgreens, the small and vibrant greens that are harvested just a few weeks after planting, have gained immense popularity in recent years due to their numerous health benefits, ease of cultivation, and suitability for indoor growing.[6]

According to research by the United States Department of Agriculture (USDA), microgreens contain up to 40 times more nutrients than their mature counterparts. For example, red cabbage microgreens contain six times more Vitamin C and 69 times more Vitamin K than mature red cabbage. Additionally, they contain high levels of antioxidants, which help fight against inflammation and reduce the risk of chronic diseases such as heart disease and cancer.[7]

Microgreens are also relatively easy to cultivate, making them an accessible option for home gardeners and urban farmers alike. They can be grown using a variety of methods, including hydroponics, soil, or even on kitchen towels. They require minimal space, water, and sunlight, and can be harvested as early as one to two weeks after planting.[8]

Furthermore, microgreens are particularly suitable for indoor growing, making them a viable option for those who live in urban areas with limited access to outdoor space. They can be grown in containers, trays or even vertical gardens, making them a perfect option for small apartment balconies or kitchens. The growing demand for locally sourced and fresh produce has led to the increased popularity of indoor growing, and microgreens are well-suited to this trend.[9]

Apart from their numerous health benefits and ease of cultivation, microgreens are also popular due to their versatility in cooking. They can be used in salads, sandwiches, and smoothies, or as a garnish for a variety of dishes. Their unique flavors range from spicy to sweet, making them a great addition to any meal.

In conclusion, microgreens have become increasingly popular due to their exceptional nutritional benefits, ease of cultivation, and suitability for indoor growing. Their high nutrient content, versatility in cooking, and minimal space requirements make them an attractive option for those looking to incorporate fresh, healthy produce into their daily diet. As more people turn to indoor growing, microgreens are likely to continue gaining popularity as a sustainable and accessible source of nutritious food.

1.3 The importance of monitoring environmental factors for indoor microgreen growth.

The popularity of indoor microgreen cultivation has grown tremendously in recent years, as people are increasingly interested in locally sourced fresh produce that can be grown year-round. However, growing microgreens indoors require careful monitoring and control of environmental factors to ensure optimal growth and yield.

One critical factor to monitor is temperature. The ideal temperature range for most microgreens is between 60°F to 75°F (15°C to 24°C). Temperatures outside of this range can lead to stunted growth or even death of the plants. For example, if the temperature is too high, the plants may bolt, which means they grow too tall too quickly and become spindly, reducing their nutritional value.[10]

Humidity is another important factor to monitor in indoor microgreen cultivation. Maintaining a humidity level of around 70-80% helps prevent the soil from drying out and ensures adequate moisture for the plants. However, excessively high humidity levels can lead to mold and fungal growth, which can damage or kill the plants.[11]

Lighting is also crucial for indoor microgreen growth. Microgreens require at least 12 hours of light per day, and artificial lighting can be used to provide consistent light levels regardless of the season or time of day. Inadequate lighting can result in poor growth, while excessive lighting can cause heat stress or leaf burn.[12]

Finally, water quality must be monitored closely when growing microgreens indoors. Tap water may contain high levels of chlorine or other chemicals that can harm plants. It's recommended to use filtered or distilled water to avoid these issues.[13][14]

In conclusion, monitoring environmental factors is vital for successful indoor microgreen cultivation. Temperature, humidity, lighting, and water quality must all be carefully controlled to ensure optimal growth and yield. With the right monitoring, indoor microgreen cultivation can provide a sustainable and nutrient-rich source of fresh produce throughout the year.

The thermistor measures the temperature by changing its resistance as the temperature changes. The microcontroller reads the changes in capacitance and resistance and converts them into digital signals, which can be used to determine the relative humidity and temperature in the environment.
